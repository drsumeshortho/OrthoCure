PRP injections for selected knee and shoulder problems

PRP uses a concentrated portion of your own blood in carefully selected joint and tendon problems. At OrthoCure, PRP is offered only after assessment and explanation of realistic benefit. It is never positioned as a cure, but as one part of a structured plan.

When PRP may help

  • Early to moderate knee arthritis
  • Shoulder tendinopathy without full thickness tear
  • Persistent pain despite exercise and medicines
  • Patients aiming to delay surgery responsibly

When PRP is unlikely to help

  • Advanced arthritis with severe deformity
  • Complete tendon or ligament tears
  • Expectation of cartilage regrowth
  • No willingness to do rehabilitation

How PRP works in simple terms

  • Platelets release factors that calm inflammation
  • Goal is pain reduction and better movement
  • Effect appears gradually over weeks
  • Response varies from person to person

What the procedure involves

  • Blood drawn and processed in clinic
  • Injection under sterile precautions
  • Short rest after the procedure
  • Clear post injection guidance

Risks and limits

  • Temporary pain or swelling for a day or two
  • Small infection risk like any injection
  • No guaranteed benefit
  • Surgery may still be needed later

Why rehabilitation matters more

  • Weight control and activity modification
  • Targeted strengthening
  • Joint protection strategies
  • Follow up and reassessment
Important: PRP is never offered as a shortcut. If simple measures or surgery are more appropriate, that is explained upfront.

Common questions

Will PRP rebuild cartilage

No. Evidence shows symptom relief in selected patients. Cartilage regrowth is not proven.

How many injections are needed

Usually one to three, planned cautiously based on response and joint involved.

Can PRP replace knee replacement

No. It may delay surgery in some patients but does not replace it when arthritis is advanced.

Footwear vs Knees

Evidence-Based Shoe Fixes to Lower Knee Pain

By Dr. Sumesh Subramanian • OrthoCure Bone & Joint Speciality Clinic, Thirumullaivoyal

What Research Says

Modern trials show that footwear is more than comfort—it alters how your knees bear load. For example:

  • Shakoor et al, 2008: “Effects of Specialized Footwear on Joint Loads in Osteoarthritis of the Knee” found that shoes designed to reduce knee adduction moment lowered joint loads by ~8-12% compared to regular walking shoes. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}
  • Reichenbach et al, 2020 (BIOTOK trial, JAMA): Biomechanical footwear (adjustable convex pods) versus control footwear improved knee pain and physical function at 24 weeks. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
  • Paterson et al, 2018: In “Footwear for self-managing knee osteoarthritis symptoms,” flat flexible shoes were associated with lower knee adduction moment vs supportive shoes. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

3 Quick Fixes to Try Today

  1. Check heel stability: shoes should resist collapse when pinched at back.
  2. Moderate sole flexibility: forefoot bend should occur at toes, not mid-arch.
  3. Avoid ultra thin/worn out soles: replace footwear every 9-12 months or when tread is visibly worn.

Typical Slipper / Flat Shoes

  • High knee adduction moment (higher pressure on inner knee)
  • Poor heel counter → risk of drift or wobble
  • Sole degrades quickly; less cushioning

Supportive / Specialty Footwear
  • Reduces peak knee load (as shown in trials) by up to 12% :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  • Better cushioning + stable heel = less strain during walking
  • More predictable wear; easier to monitor replacement timing

Buying Checklist

  • Firm heel counter resistant to collapse
  • Midfoot support or neutral motion control
  • Forefoot region bends only at toes
  • Shock absorption but not overly heavy
  • Proper fit (room for longest toe + half thumbwidth spacing)

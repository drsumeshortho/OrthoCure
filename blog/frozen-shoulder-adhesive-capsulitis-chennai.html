What is frozen shoulder?

The shoulder is a ball and socket joint wrapped by a soft sleeve called the capsule. In frozen shoulder, the capsule becomes inflamed, thick, and tight. The joint space effectively feels smaller, so the shoulder cannot glide freely.

2 to 5%
Frozen shoulder affects a small but important portion of adults, often between 40 and 65 years. Diabetes and thyroid disorders increase risk and may slow recovery.

Why is frozen shoulder worse at night?

Night pain is a classic symptom. People describe deep aching pain, inability to sleep on the affected side, and repeated sleep breaks.

What is happening inside the shoulder?

When the arm stays still for long periods, the inflamed capsule becomes more sensitive. Lying down can increase pressure around the shoulder, especially if you roll onto that side. That is why night pain can feel worse even when daytime pain seems tolerable.

Who gets frozen shoulder, and why does diabetes matter?

Frozen shoulder can affect anyone, but these factors increase risk:

  • Age between 40 and 65 years
  • Diabetes mellitus, especially long standing or poorly controlled
  • Thyroid disease
  • Prolonged immobilisation after injury or surgery
  • Previous frozen shoulder on the other side

For diabetic frozen shoulder, sugar control is part of treatment. If sugars are running high, the capsule tends to stay irritable longer, and stiffness can linger.

Stages and a realistic timeline: what to expect

Frozen shoulder usually progresses through three overlapping stages. Knowing your stage prevents panic and prevents forcing the wrong exercise at the wrong time.

Stage What you feel What usually helps most
Freezing Increasing pain, strong night pain, stiffness slowly builds Pain control first, gentle movement only
Frozen Pain may reduce, stiffness feels dominant, daily tasks become difficult Consistency with stretching and guided exercises
Thawing Pain stays low, movement improves gradually over months Progressive strengthening and functional recovery

One common mistake: forcing stretches too early

Aggressive stretching during the painful freezing stage can flare inflammation and increase night pain. The right plan matches the stage, not just the scan.

How do we confirm the diagnosis?

Frozen shoulder is mainly a clinical diagnosis. In true frozen shoulder, both active movement and passive movement are limited in more than one direction.

  • Clinical exam to confirm global restriction with a firm end feel
  • X ray to rule out arthritis or bone causes of pain
  • Ultrasound or MRI only if another issue is suspected, for example rotator cuff tear
  • Blood tests sometimes for diabetes and thyroid status

Non surgical treatment: what actually works

Most patients improve with a conservative stepwise plan. The priorities are pain control, safe range of motion, and gradual return of function.

Step 1: Pain control and smart activity changes

  • Avoid painful end range forcing
  • Use heat or ice based on comfort
  • Short safe medicines when appropriate
  • Sleep position guidance to reduce night pain

Step 2: Guided physiotherapy and a simple home routine

Random exercises from the internet often fail because they ignore stage and technique. A simple routine done daily usually beats occasional intense sessions.

Step 3: Injections for selected patients

In selected cases, an injection can reduce capsule inflammation and make exercises easier, especially in the painful stage. It is not mandatory for everyone, and it is never a stand alone fix.

When is surgery considered?

Surgery is considered only when clear criteria are met, usually after months of proper conservative care.

  • Severe stiffness persists despite supervised therapy and a good home routine
  • Daily function remains significantly limited
  • Other causes have been assessed and addressed

Simple truth

Most patients do not need surgery. The goal is the right plan at the right time, not the fastest plan.

“The night pain was the worst part. Once I understood the condition and followed regular exercises, my sleep slowly improved and the shoulder loosened over time.”

Frequently asked questions

Can frozen shoulder affect both shoulders?

Yes. Many people start with one side and later develop similar symptoms on the other side, especially in diabetes. Early guidance and consistency reduce severity.

Will my shoulder become completely normal again?

Many patients regain near normal function for daily life. Some may have mild limitation at extreme end range, which often does not affect routine tasks.

What if I cannot attend regular physiotherapy sessions?

A well taught home plan with periodic reviews can still work well. The key is correct technique, consistency, and stage appropriate progress.

When should I see an orthopaedic doctor?

If pain disturbs sleep, movement is steadily reducing, symptoms persist beyond a few weeks, or you have diabetes with shoulder stiffness, it is safer to get assessed early.

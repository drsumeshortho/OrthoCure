What is Frozen Shoulder

The shoulder joint is like a ball and socket wrapped in a loose sleeve called the capsule. In Frozen Shoulder this capsule becomes inflamed, thick, and tight. The inside space shrinks, and sticky bands form. The joint simply cannot glide the way it should.

Pain and stiffness are out of proportion to any visible X ray changes. This is why many patients are confused when their reports look almost normal yet everyday tasks are difficult.

Who gets Frozen Shoulder

Any adult can develop Frozen Shoulder, but some people are clearly at higher risk.

  • Age between forty and seventy years.
  • Diabetes, especially if long standing or poorly controlled.
  • Thyroid disease, both underactive and overactive states.
  • Recent shoulder injury, fracture, or surgery with prolonged immobilisation.
  • Prolonged bed rest or arm support after heart or chest procedures.
  • Other metabolic conditions or autoimmune disease.

Symptoms and the three stages of Frozen Shoulder

The classic features are deep shoulder pain, difficulty lying on that side, and stiff movement in almost every direction. Wearing a blouse or shirt, combing hair, reaching into the back seat of a car, or putting on a bra become difficult.

Frozen Shoulder usually progresses through three overlapping stages. These are not strict boxes but they help you understand what is happening.

Stage What you feel Typical duration Clinical notes
Freezing stage Strong pain, especially at night and at the end of movement. Stiffness slowly increasing. Three to eight months Inflammation in the capsule is active. Aim is pain control and gentle protected movement.
Frozen stage Pain gradually reduces, stiffness is the main complaint. Range is markedly limited. Four to twelve months Capsule is thick and tight. Focus is on stretching, physiotherapy, and function.
Thawing stage Pain is much less. Movement slowly improves, first in daily tasks then in overhead reach. Six to eighteen months Gradual remodelling of capsule. Consistent exercises help regain strength and range.

How Frozen Shoulder is diagnosed

Diagnosis is mainly clinical. During an examination I look for a reduction in both active and passive movements in more than one direction, together with characteristic pain.

Tests often used include:

  • X ray of the shoulder to rule out arthritis, fracture, or dislocation. In pure Frozen Shoulder this is usually normal.
  • Ultrasound if we suspect a rotator cuff tear, bursitis, or significant calcium deposit.
  • Blood tests in selected patients, especially when diabetes, thyroid disease, or inflammatory arthritis are suspected.

Treatment without surgery

The good news is that many people improve with non surgical care, especially when treatment is started early and tailored to the stage of the condition and to diabetes status.

Pain control and rest that is sensible

  • Short courses of pain relieving medicines and anti inflammatory drugs where safe, after checking other health conditions.
  • A well fitted sling in the very painful phase for short periods, but not all day. Complete rest slows recovery.
  • Night support with a pillow under the arm and attention to sleep, as outlined in our article Why Your Joints Need Good Sleep to Heal.

Structured physiotherapy and home exercises

Once pain is reasonably controlled, guided stretching and gradual strengthening are the main treatment. Random movements or aggressive forcing can flare the capsule and set you back.

  • Pendulum swings with the trunk supported.
  • Wall crawl in forward and side directions.
  • Passive external rotation stretches using a stick.
  • Later, isometric and then resisted strengthening for rotator cuff and scapular muscles.

The plan is always adjusted for pain, stage, and diabetes control. For many working adults in Ambattur and Avadi, we build a routine that fits into early morning or late evening schedules.

Injections and other procedures

In selected cases, a steroid injection into the joint under guidance can reduce capsule inflammation and cut pain, especially in the freezing stage. This makes physiotherapy easier to start. In some cases, a distension procedure with fluid is considered to stretch the capsule.

Injections are not a shortcut and they are not repeated casually. They are most useful as one part of a larger plan that includes movement, strength work, and good blood sugar control. When is surgery considered

Surgery is not the first line in Frozen Shoulder. It is considered only when:

  • Stiffness remains severe after many months of well supervised physiotherapy.
  • Pain and limit in movement clearly affect work and self care.
  • Other causes like rotator cuff tear have been evaluated and addressed.

Options include manipulation under anaesthesia or arthroscopic capsular release. Both need close post procedure physiotherapy. The decision is very individual and depends on age, diabetes control, work demands, and response to earlier treatment.

Living with Frozen Shoulder in Chennai

While the condition runs its natural course, simple daily adjustments reduce strain and keep you as independent as possible.

  • Use the other arm for heavy lifting, but do not completely neglect the affected side.
  • Adjust work and kitchen shelves so that frequently used items are between chest and waist height.
  • Avoid sudden jerks, heavy overhead loads, and long periods with the arm hanging unsupported.
  • Use public transport or bike handles with care; avoid sudden pulls and pushes in traffic.
  • Plan short, frequent exercise breaks rather than one long session.

Principles of sensible loading and graded activity are similar to the PEACE and LOVE strategy for injuries explained in our article Why PEACE and LOVE is Replacing RICE in Injury Recovery.

When to see your orthopaedic doctor

See a specialist without delay if:

  • Pain has lasted more than four to six weeks and is not improving.
  • Night pain wakes you from sleep often.
  • You cannot lift the arm to shoulder level or reach behind your back.
  • You have diabetes or thyroid disease and shoulder pain that is not explained.
  • There is sudden loss of power, recent fall, or obvious deformity.

Frequently asked questions

Can Frozen Shoulder affect both sides

Yes. Most people start with one shoulder, but the other side can be affected later, especially in diabetes. This is another reason to keep blood sugar controlled and shoulder joints moving.

Will my shoulder become completely normal again

Many patients regain near normal use for daily activities. Some may have mild loss at the end of range, such as extreme reach, but this rarely limits normal life if the program is followed well. The key is patience and consistency through the stages.

Is Frozen Shoulder permanent damage

In true Adhesive Capsulitis, changes are largely soft tissue based and can remodel over time. This is different from advanced arthritis where cartilage is worn out. Pathway and prognosis are different.

What if I cannot do physiotherapy regularly

Even if formal sessions are limited, a clearly explained home program with periodic review can help. In our clinic we often train patients and family members together so that support at home is practical.

Key takeaways

  • Frozen Shoulder is common, especially in people with diabetes between forty and seventy years.
  • It progresses through freezing, frozen, and thawing stages over months.
  • Diagnosis is clinical; X rays are mainly to rule out other problems.
  • Most people improve with medicines, guided physiotherapy, and selected injections.
  • Surgery is reserved for persistent, disabling stiffness despite good non surgical care.
  • Education, realistic expectations, and steady exercises matter more than shortcuts.

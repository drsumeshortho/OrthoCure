What exactly is Frozen Shoulder

The shoulder joint is a ball and socket wrapped by a soft sleeve called the capsule. In Frozen Shoulder this capsule becomes inflamed, thick and tight. The inside space shrinks and the joint can no longer glide freely. Pain and stiffness appear together, often without a big injury.

Frozen Shoulder is most common between forty and seventy years. It affects about two to five percent of adults. In people with diabetes, the risk can be several times higher and the course often more stubborn.

Who gets Frozen Shoulder and why diabetes matters

Anyone can develop Frozen Shoulder, but some groups are clearly more at risk.

  • Age between forty and seventy years.
  • Diabetes, especially if long standing or poorly controlled.
  • Thyroid disease, both underactive and overactive states.
  • Shoulder kept in sling or unused for long after fracture or surgery.
  • Prolonged bed rest or chest procedures needing arm support.
  • Previous Frozen Shoulder on the other side.

Why the shoulder and diabetes are linked

High blood sugar over time affects collagen and soft tissue. The capsule around the shoulder becomes less elastic and more prone to thickening and inflammation. This is why we always look at your sugars before planning injections, physiotherapy intensity, or surgery.

The three stages of Frozen Shoulder

Frozen Shoulder does not appear or disappear overnight. It passes through three overlapping stages. Understanding this prevents both panic and unrealistic promises.

Stage What you usually feel Typical duration
Freezing stage Strong pain, especially at night. Sudden catch of pain at the end of movement. Stiffness slowly building. Three to eight months
Frozen stage Pain gradually reduces. Shoulder feels stuck. Daily activities like dressing and combing hair are difficult. Four to twelve months
Thawing stage Pain low. Movement slowly improves. You notice small gains over weeks and months. Six to eighteen months

A word of caution

Very aggressive stretching in the early painful stage can flare inflammation and set you back. Treatment has to match the stage, not just the X ray or one time pain score.

How we diagnose Frozen Shoulder

Diagnosis is mainly clinical. During an assessment, I look at how much you can move actively and how much the joint moves when I move it for you. In true Frozen Shoulder, both are limited in more than one direction.

Clinical examination
Global loss of movement in forward elevation, rotation and reaching behind, often with a firm end feel and typical pain pattern.
Imaging
Shoulder X ray to rule out arthritis or other bone problems. Ultrasound or MRI only if rotator cuff tear, bursitis, or other pathology is suspected.
Blood work when needed
Diabetes and thyroid profiles, sometimes markers of inflammation, based on your overall story.

Treatment without rushing to surgery

Most cases of Frozen Shoulder improve with non surgical care when treatment is started early and matched to the stage. The core approach at OrthoCure is simple and disciplined.

Pain control that still allows movement

Short, carefully chosen courses of pain relief and anti inflammatory medicines where safe. Simple measures like ice packs or heat packs are used depending on stage and comfort.

Structured physiotherapy and home exercises

Random exercises from the internet rarely work. We usually recommend:

  • Pendulum swings with proper trunk support.
  • Wall slides in front and side directions within tolerable pain.
  • Stick assisted external rotation and gentle stretches behind the back.
  • Later, gradual strengthening for rotator cuff and shoulder blade muscles.

Injections and supported procedures

In selected cases, a steroid injection into the joint can reduce capsule inflammation and improve pain, especially in the freezing stage. Distension procedures with fluid are considered in specific situations. In all cases, injections are one part of a broader exercise based plan, not a stand alone fix.

When do we consider surgery

Surgery is considered only when clear criteria are met, for example:

  • Severe stiffness remains after many months of supervised physiotherapy.
  • Pain and restriction continue to interfere with work and personal care.
  • Other causes like rotator cuff tear have been assessed and addressed.

Conservative care

Focus on pain control, stretching, strengthening, and metabolic control. Recovery is gradual but avoids surgical risk. This is the first line for almost all patients.

Surgical options

Manipulation under anaesthesia or arthroscopic capsular release. Useful for selected patients, but still needs strict physiotherapy afterwards and is never the first step by default.

Living with Frozen Shoulder in Chennai

While the shoulder is recovering, small lifestyle adjustments can protect the joint and keep you independent.

Simple day to day adjustments

  • Use the other arm for heavy lifting but do not completely neglect the affected side.
  • Keep commonly used kitchen and office items between chest and waist height.
  • Avoid sudden jerks, heavy overhead loads, and long rides with the arm unsupported.
  • Break your exercise into shorter, frequent sets spread through the day.
  • Protect your sleep. Good night rest is a powerful pain modulator; see Why Your Joints Need Good Sleep to Heal.

Frequently asked questions

Can Frozen Shoulder affect both shoulders

Yes. Many people start with one side and later develop similar symptoms on the other side, especially if they have diabetes. Early attention to shoulder movement and blood sugar control can reduce severity.

Will my shoulder become completely normal again

Many patients regain near normal function for daily life. Some may have mild limitation at the extreme end of range, such as very high reach, which often does not interfere with routine tasks when exercises are followed well.

Is Frozen Shoulder permanent damage

True Adhesive Capsulitis mainly affects soft tissue, not the bone surfaces. With time and movement, the capsule can remodel. This is different from advanced arthritis, where cartilage loss is permanent.

What if I cannot attend regular physiotherapy sessions

Even if formal sessions are limited, a clearly explained home program with periodic review can still help. In our clinic we often teach simple routines that fit into a busy schedule and involve family support when needed.

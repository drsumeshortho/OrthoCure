What is frozen shoulder?

Frozen shoulder, also called adhesive capsulitis, happens when the soft capsule around the shoulder becomes inflamed, thick, and tight. This reduces the space inside the joint, so the shoulder cannot glide freely. Movement becomes painful and restricted in more than one direction.

The reassuring truth

Most cases improve without surgery when managed correctly and patiently. A stage based plan is usually more calming and more effective than simply waiting it out.

Who is more likely to get frozen shoulder?

Frozen shoulder can affect anyone, but these groups are at higher risk.

  • Age group 40 to 65 years
  • Diabetes mellitus, especially long standing or poorly controlled
  • Thyroid disease
  • Prolonged immobilisation after injury or surgery
  • Previous frozen shoulder on the other side
Doctor’s note for diabetic patients

Blood sugar control plays a key role in recovery. In diabetic frozen shoulder, the shoulder and the metabolism both need attention.

Common symptoms patients notice

These are the patterns I hear most often from patients in Thirumullaivoyal and nearby areas.

  • Pain that worsens at night and breaks sleep
  • Progressive stiffness and reduced reach
  • Difficulty dressing, wearing a bra, combing hair, or reaching behind the back
  • Daily activities feel difficult even without major injury

Why frozen shoulder pain is worse at night?

Night pain is common because the inflamed capsule becomes more sensitive when the arm stays still for long periods. Pressure around the shoulder can also increase while lying down, especially if you lie on the affected side.

Stillness increases sensitivity

When movement reduces, the inflamed capsule feels more irritable, especially during sleep.

Pressure from lying down

Even small pressure changes can trigger deep aching pain in an already sensitive joint.

The three stages of frozen shoulder

Frozen shoulder usually progresses through three overlapping stages. Knowing the stage prevents panic and prevents over stretching. The goal is steady progress, not forced progress.

Stage 1: Freezing

What you feel
Increasing pain, strong night pain, stiffness slowly builds.

What helps most
Pain control, gentle movement only, avoid forceful stretching.

Stage 2: Frozen

What you feel
Pain may reduce, stiffness dominates, daily tasks become difficult.

What helps most
Consistency with guided stretching and a simple daily routine.

Stage 3: Thawing

What you feel
Pain is lower, movement improves gradually over months.

What helps most
Progressive strengthening and functional recovery.

One common mistake

Forceful stretching early can flare inflammation and increase night pain. Treatment should match the stage, not just the X ray.

Treatment without surgery

Most patients improve with a conservative, stepwise plan. The priorities are pain control, safe range of motion, and gradual function.

Step 1: Control pain and modify activity

  • Avoid painful end range forcing
  • Use heat or ice based on comfort
  • Short, appropriate medicines when suitable
  • Sleep position guidance to reduce night pain

Step 2: Guided physiotherapy and a home routine

Random exercises from the internet often fail because they ignore stage and technique. A simple routine done daily usually beats occasional intense sessions.

Step 3: Injections if needed

In selected cases, an injection can reduce capsule inflammation and make exercises easier, especially in the painful stage. It is not mandatory for everyone and it is never a stand alone fix.

Our guiding principle

Recovery without surgery. A stage based plan is more calming and more effective than simply waiting it out.

What recovery realistically looks like

Many patients regain near normal function for daily life. Some may have a mild limitation at the extreme end of motion, which often does not affect routine tasks.

The goal is steady progress. Sleep often improves first, then pain reduces, then movement improves.

When to seek help

  • Night pain is disturbing sleep
  • Movement is steadily reducing
  • Symptoms persist beyond 4 to 6 weeks
  • You have diabetes with shoulder pain or stiffness

Frequently asked questions

Can frozen shoulder affect both shoulders?

Yes. It can occur on the other side as well, especially in diabetes. Early guidance can reduce severity.

Will my shoulder become completely normal again?

Many patients regain near normal function for daily life. Some may have mild limitation at extreme end range.

Do I need an MRI?

Not always. Diagnosis is mainly clinical. MRI is usually needed only if another problem is suspected.

What if I cannot attend regular physiotherapy?

A well taught home plan with periodic reviews can still work very well. Consistency matters more than intensity.

What is frozen shoulder

Frozen shoulder occurs when the capsule around the shoulder joint becomes inflamed, thickened and tight. This reduces the joint space and restricts movement in multiple directions.

Image placeholder: Shoulder capsule tightening illustration

Why pain gets worse at night

Night pain is a classic feature. When the arm remains still for long periods, inflammation inside the capsule becomes more noticeable. Lying on the affected side also increases pressure, disturbing sleep.

Image placeholder: Night pain and sleep disturbance illustration

Who is at higher risk

  • Age between 40 and 65 years
  • Diabetes mellitus (especially poorly controlled)
  • Thyroid disorders
  • Prolonged immobilisation after injury or surgery
In people with diabetes, frozen shoulder may last longer unless blood sugar control is addressed alongside shoulder treatment.

Stages of frozen shoulder

Stage What you notice
Freezing Increasing pain, especially at night, gradual stiffness
Frozen Less pain, marked stiffness affecting daily tasks
Thawing Gradual improvement in movement over months
Image placeholder: Frozen shoulder stages timeline graphic

Treatment approach

Treatment depends on the stage. Forcing movements too early can worsen pain. A structured plan usually includes:

  • Pain control and activity modification
  • Stage-appropriate physiotherapy
  • Home exercises done consistently
  • Injections only when clearly indicated

When is surgery considered

Surgery is rarely required. It is considered only if stiffness remains severe after months of proper conservative treatment.

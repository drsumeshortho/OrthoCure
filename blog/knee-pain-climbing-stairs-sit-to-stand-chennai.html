Knee Pain While Climbing Stairs and Getting Up From a Chair

If your knee pain is worse on stairs or when standing up from a chair, it often points to a load and movement control issue rather than serious joint damage.

Explained clearly by Dr. Sumesh Subramanian, Orthopaedic Surgeon, OrthoCure Bone and Joint Speciality Clinic, Thirumullaivoyal.

Quick question: Does your pain sit mainly in the front of the knee, feel worse on stairs, but improve on flat walking?

If yes, this guide is for you.

Knee pain on stairs or while getting up from a chair is one of the most common problems I see in daily practice at OrthoCure, especially among patients from Thirumullaivoyal, Ambattur, and Avadi.

The two biggest fears patients express are simple:
“Is this arthritis?” and “Will I need surgery?”

In many cases, the answer to both is no.

Why does knee pain worsen on stairs and chair rise?

When the knee bends under load, the kneecap presses firmly against the thigh bone. Movements like stair climbing, squatting, and standing up from a chair significantly increase this pressure.

If hip and thigh muscles are not supporting the movement efficiently, extra stress concentrates at the front of the knee. This pain pattern is commonly called patellofemoral pain.

  • Stairs require body weight support in a bent-knee position.
  • Low chairs and sofas increase joint stress during standing up.
  • Floor-level sitting and Indian toilets add further strain.

Is this always arthritis?

No. Many patients with front knee pain have normal X-rays. Arthritis is more likely when pain is associated with stiffness, swelling, or discomfort even during flat walking.

A focused clinical examination usually clarifies the pattern quickly, without rushing into unnecessary scans.

What actually helps?

Most patients improve with a structured, non-surgical plan that includes:

  • Education about load management.
  • Strengthening of hip and thigh muscles.
  • Gradual return to stairs with better control.
  • Short-term pain relief only as support, not as the main solution.

Surgery is rarely required for this pain pattern.

When should you see an orthopaedic doctor?

  • If pain persists beyond two to three weeks.
  • If swelling, locking, or repeated giving way occurs.
  • If pain worsens despite sensible activity modification.
  • If you want clarity instead of repeated trial-and-error.

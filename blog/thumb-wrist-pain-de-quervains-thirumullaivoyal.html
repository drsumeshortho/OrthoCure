Thumb and Wrist Pain from Repetitive Use

From lifting a baby to gripping a two wheeler handle or tools at work — pain at the base of the thumb is usually a tendon problem, not a bone injury, and it often responds well to conservative care.

What it is and why it happens

The thumb moves using two tendons that run in a shared sheath along the thumb side of the wrist. When these tendons are used repeatedly in a gripping, pinching, or twisting motion, the sheath can become tight and inflamed — a condition called De Quervain's tenosynovitis.

The inflammation makes every movement of the thumb painful because the tendons have to squeeze through a narrowed, irritated tunnel every time they move.

Doctor's note: Most people assume this is a bone problem or early arthritis. In many cases it is a tendon overuse injury. The encouraging part: tendon irritation responds well to the right conservative care and rarely needs surgery.

Common triggers in daily life

New mothers and childcare Frequently lifting an infant with the thumb extended and wrist bent is one of the most common triggers. Hormonal changes after delivery also make tendons more vulnerable to irritation — which is why many people call this "mommy thumb."
Manual work and tool use Repetitive grasping and twisting during assembly work, carpentry, electrical work, or mechanics can overload the same tendon area. Factory workers around the Ambattur Industrial Estate frequently present with this pattern.
Two wheeler commuting in Chennai Constant throttle grip, frequent clutch and brake action, and road vibration can aggravate the thumb side of the wrist — especially for riders commuting from Avadi, Annanur, and Ambattur daily.
Home and office strain Wringing clothes, lifting heavy vessels, prolonged phone holding in one hand, and repetitive mouse use can all trigger or worsen the same pain pattern.

Typical symptoms

You may notice:

  • Pain near the base of the thumb, on the thumb side of the wrist.
  • Pain that worsens with gripping, twisting, or lifting.
  • Swelling or tenderness along the tendon running to the thumb.
  • A feeling of catching or snapping when moving the thumb.
  • Pain while picking up a child, a bag, or a water bottle.

Quick self check

Ask yourself these four questions:

  • Does pain increase when you grip a handle, throttle, or twist a cloth?
  • Does pain worsen when you lift a child or a heavy object?
  • Is the pain mainly on the thumb side of the wrist, not across the whole hand?
  • Does short rest improve it slightly, but pain returns the moment you grip again?

If most answers are yes, this is very likely a tendon overuse problem. A brief clinical examination — not a scan — is usually all that is needed to confirm it and rule out other causes like a fracture or nerve compression.

Step by step relief plan

The goal is to calm the inflammation, protect the tendons while they settle, and then gradually restore strength. Surgery is the last resort — most patients recover well without it.

  1. Identify the trigger and modify the grip Reduce repetitive gripping for a short period. Switch hands when possible. For two wheeler riders: avoid a tight death-grip on the throttle and keep the wrists in a neutral, relaxed position. For new mothers, experiment with different carrying positions to reduce thumb load.
  2. Ice and short term pain control An ice pack applied for 10 minutes, 2 to 3 times daily, helps reduce local swelling. Short-term medication may be used when needed, based on clinical advice. Do not take anti-inflammatories continuously without a review.
  3. Thumb spica splint A thumb spica splint holds the thumb and wrist in a resting position so the irritated tendons stop being rubbed with every movement. Consistent splint use during daily activities — especially riding and lifting — makes a significant difference. We can advise the right type and fitting for your hand.
  4. Guided exercises and physiotherapy Once pain begins to reduce, gentle tendon gliding exercises and progressive strengthening help restore full movement and prevent recurrence. Our physiotherapy team at OrthoCure can build a plan that fits your work and commute. Learn about physiotherapy in Thirumullaivoyal.
  5. If pain persists — injection or further review Some patients benefit from a corticosteroid injection into the tendon sheath when splinting and exercises have not produced enough relief. This settles the inflammation so rehabilitation can progress. Surgery is considered only for the rare stubborn cases that do not respond to proper conservative care over time.

When to worry

Please seek a clinical review promptly if you have:

  • Numbness or tingling in the fingers or hand.
  • Severe weakness or dropping objects unexpectedly.
  • Visible swelling that does not reduce at all with rest.
  • History of a fall or injury to the wrist or hand.
  • Pain lasting more than 2 to 3 weeks despite rest and splinting.
  • Night pain that wakes you from sleep.

These features suggest the issue may not be simple tendon irritation and needs a proper clinical assessment to rule out fracture, nerve compression, or other conditions.

What we examine in clinic

Before ordering any scans, we use clinical tests to narrow the cause. This saves you time and expense and is usually all that is needed.

  • Site of tenderness — exactly where along the thumb side of the wrist the pain is coming from.
  • Finkelstein's test — a simple in-clinic movement test that specifically stresses the De Quervain's tendons.
  • Movement assessment — range of thumb and wrist movement compared to the other side.
  • Nerve screening — if there is any tingling or numbness, we check for carpal tunnel or other nerve issues.

Frequently asked questions

  • Is thumb base pain always arthritis?
    No. Tendon overuse is a very common cause, especially with repetitive gripping and lifting. Arthritis is one possibility but a clinical examination — not a scan — usually tells the difference quickly.
  • Do I need an MRI for thumb and wrist pain?
    Usually no. A clinical examination with targeted movement tests is often enough. An MRI is reserved for cases where diagnosis is unclear or conservative care has not worked after several weeks.
  • How long does it take to improve?
    Many patients improve meaningfully in 2 to 6 weeks with a thumb spica splint, activity modification, and guided exercises. Recovery is faster when the trigger activity is controlled early.
  • What is mommy thumb?
    It is a common name for De Quervain's tenosynovitis that develops in new mothers from repeatedly lifting an infant with an extended thumb. The same condition affects two wheeler riders, factory workers, and anyone with a repetitive grip demand.
  • Is surgery common for De Quervain's?
    No. Surgery is considered only when conservative care — splinting, exercises, and if needed an injection — has genuinely not worked over an adequate period. Most patients do not reach that stage.

What Exactly Is Sciatica?

Sciatica is a symptom—not a disease—caused by irritation or compression of the sciatic nerve or its lumbar nerve roots (commonly L4–S1). The most frequent cause is a lumbar disc herniation, but spinal stenosis, facet joint cysts, or inflammation can also play a role. Because the symptom profile overlaps with other conditions, a careful clinical evaluation is essential. Residents of Avadi who visit OrthoCure Bone & Joint Clinic Thirumullaivoyal are assessed for red flags such as severe weakness, bladder/bowel changes, fever, or unexplained weight loss—findings that require urgent attention.

Diagnosis: Careful History, Focused Examination, Sensible Imaging

Diagnosis begins with a focused history (onset, radiation, numbness/weakness, aggravating positions) and a neurological exam (reflexes, power, sensation, straight-leg raise). Imaging is not always necessary at the start. In many cases, we recommend a trial of conservative care first. MRI is considered if red flags are present, if symptoms persist beyond several weeks despite appropriate therapy, or if injection/surgical planning is required. What You Can Do at Home (Guideline-Aligned)

  • Active rest: Keep moving within comfort. Short walks at home are better than long hours on the bed or sofa.
  • Heat or cold packs: Heat may ease muscle spasm; cold can reduce inflammatory soreness. Try each for 10–15 minutes and use what feels best.
  • Anti-inflammatory foods & hydration: A balanced diet, adequate water, and limiting processed foods can support recovery.
  • Avoid heavy lifting & prolonged sitting: Use lumbar support and change posture frequently, especially if commuting from Avadi to the city.
  • Sleep smart: Side-lying with a pillow between the knees or on your back with a pillow under the knees can reduce strain.

Medication: Relief Without Over-Reliance

Medicine helps you move, and movement helps you heal. The major guidelines agree on a few points:

  • First-line: short courses of NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen or naproxen) for pain and inflammation, provided there are no contraindications such as kidney disease, ulcers, or interactions.
  • Oral steroids: not supported by high-quality evidence for routine use in acute sciatica.
  • Opioids (e.g., tramadol): generally not recommended for chronic low-back or sciatic pain due to limited benefit and dependency risk.

Physiotherapy: The Engine of Recovery

When pain persists beyond a couple of weeks—or if it recurs—guided physiotherapy becomes central. Evidence supports early, structured rehabilitation emphasizing:

Nerve glides (neural mobilization): Gentle movements that help irritated nerves slide freely, reducing sensitivity without forcing painful range.
Core stabilization: Progressive activation of deep abdominal and back muscles improves spinal control for walking, lifting, and daily tasks.
Directional preference / McKenzie-style drills: Carefully selected movements (often extension-biased) that centralize pain for some patients.

Our physiotherapists at OrthoCure Bone & Joint Clinic Thirumullaivoyal teach a compact, customized routine you can continue at home in Avadi. We progress intensity week by week, aiming for better function—not just fewer symptoms.

Injections: A Bridge, Not a Destination

For patients with severe radicular pain who do not respond to medicines and initial therapy, a targeted epidural steroid injection (ESI) can provide short-term relief (often a few months). The purpose is to create a “window of opportunity” to return to walking and exercises, not to replace rehabilitation. When to See a Doctor Urgently

  • Sudden or progressive leg weakness.
  • New problems with bladder or bowel control.
  • Severe, unrelenting pain at night or unexplained fever/weight loss.
  • Recent significant trauma or cancer history.

Why We Prioritize Conservative Care

Most acute and subacute sciatica improves in 4–6 weeks with conservative care—active rest, appropriate medicines, and a structured physiotherapy plan. Surgery is reserved for clear red-flag scenarios or persistent, disabling pain that has not responded to well-executed non-operative care.

How common orthopaedic problems are approached

Different symptoms need different pathways, but the general approach remains the same: examine first, understand the likely cause, and start with the least invasive option that makes sense.

Knee pain and knee arthritis

Knee pain does not automatically mean arthritis, and knee arthritis does not automatically mean knee replacement. Treatment usually begins with examination, activity guidance, strengthening, weight advice when relevant, and short term medicines if needed. Surgery is discussed only when pain, stiffness, function, and imaging all point in the same direction.

Back pain and neck pain

Most back pain and neck pain are mechanical and improve with reassurance, posture correction, safe activity, and time. MRI is not usually required on the first visit unless there are red flags, persistent nerve symptoms, or findings that change treatment decisions.

Shoulder pain and frozen shoulder

Shoulder pain has several causes, and frozen shoulder usually needs guidance rather than panic. Pain control, phase appropriate exercises, and physiotherapy form the mainstay of treatment. Surgery is uncommon for most routine shoulder stiffness problems.

Fracture care

Not all fractures need surgery. Stability, alignment, swelling, joint involvement, and expected function all matter. Some fractures heal well in plaster or splint support with careful follow up, while others need fixation because conservative treatment would not restore safe alignment or function.

Elderly joint pain and mobility problems

Joint pain in older adults is often multifactorial. The goal is not simply pain relief. The goal is preserving walking ability, confidence, balance, independence, and comfort in daily life. Tests and procedures are used selectively and thoughtfully.

What is usually avoided at OrthoCure

A calm and careful approach also means avoiding unnecessary escalation.

What is usually avoided
  • Surgery being recommended casually on the first visit without clear indication.
  • MRI being ordered automatically on day one for every neck pain, back pain, or knee pain problem.
  • Fear based statements that create panic rather than understanding.
  • Knee replacement being suggested for early arthritis without structured conservative treatment.
  • Patients being rushed into decisions they do not fully understand.

When a second opinion helps

A second opinion is often useful when surgery has been advised quickly, imaging findings sound alarming but symptoms are manageable, or you are unsure whether conservative options were properly explored.

Frequently asked questions

Does every knee pain problem need surgery?
No. Many knee pain problems improve with structured conservative treatment such as exercises, activity modification, and weight guidance when relevant. Surgery is considered only when symptoms and findings clearly justify it.
Is MRI required for back pain on the first visit?
In most cases, no. MRI is not needed on the first visit unless there are red flag symptoms, significant nerve findings, or persistent symptoms that genuinely change treatment decisions.
Do all fractures need surgery?
No. Some fractures heal very well with plaster or splint support and careful follow up. Surgery is advised only when stability, alignment, or function make it necessary.
What is conservative orthopaedic care?
Conservative orthopaedic care means starting with the least invasive treatment that is appropriate, such as explanation, medicines when needed, exercises, physiotherapy, splints, or selected injections before moving to surgery.
When is a second opinion useful?
A second opinion is useful when surgery has been advised quickly, scan findings sound worrying but symptoms remain manageable, or you are unsure whether conservative options were properly explored.
